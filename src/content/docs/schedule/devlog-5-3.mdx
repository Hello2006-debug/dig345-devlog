**Statement:**

Bridgerton Music Box is an interactive music learning game inspired by the classical string quartet covers featured in Netflix's Bridgerton. The experience invites users to explore four seasons of the show, each with its own color palette and song selection drawn from the show's iconic covers of modern pop songs.

Built with HTML, Bootstrap, and Tone.js, the app uses a triangle wave synthesizer to recreate a warm, music-box aesthetic. Users can listen to melodies play automatically or follow along by pressing keyboard keys mapped to musical notes. A row of note dots lights up in sequence — turning green for correct notes and flashing red for mistakes — creating an immediate feedback loop between player and sound.

The project explores how familiar pop melodies can be reimagined through a Regency-era lens, connecting contemporary music culture to historical aesthetics through interactive play.

## Reading Response: Music Mouse Explained | Laurie Spiegel on Algorithmic Composition (1987)

### Summary

In this 1987 archival recording, composer and programmer Laurie Spiegel demonstrates and explains *Music Mouse*, a software instrument she developed for the Macintosh computer. Rather than a traditional composition tool, Music Mouse is designed as an intelligent instrument — one that constrains the user's input into musically coherent output using algorithmic rules. Spiegel moves the mouse across the screen, and the software maps cursor position to pitch, harmony, and rhythm in real time. The system enforces tonal relationships so that nearly any movement produces something that sounds "musical." Spiegel argues that the computer is not replacing the composer, but rather acting as a collaborator — handling the low-level rule-following so the human can focus on expression and gesture. She reflects on how algorithmic systems can democratize music-making by lowering the technical barrier to entry without eliminating creativity or personal voice.

---

### Quote

> "The instrument does a lot of the work of keeping things sounding okay, so that you can focus on the more expressive aspects of what you're doing."
> — Laurie Spiegel, *Music Mouse Explained* (1987)

---

### Comment

What strikes me most about Music Mouse is how ahead of its time the concept was. Spiegel is essentially describing what we now call *constrained interaction design* — the idea that well-designed systems don't just give users unlimited freedom, but shape that freedom in ways that produce good outcomes. This philosophy shows up everywhere today, from auto-tune to Strudel's pattern system to the way GarageBand's smart instruments work. The constraint isn't a limitation; it's part of the instrument's voice.

---

### Discussion Question

Spiegel's Music Mouse makes musical decisions *for* the user by enforcing harmonic rules. At what point does algorithmic assistance stop empowering the user and start replacing their creative agency — and who gets to decide where that line is?

### Related Work

**Patatap** (2014) by Jono Brandel — [patatap.com](https://patatap.com)

Patatap is an interactive audio-visual instrument that maps keyboard keys to animated shapes and sounds. Like Music Mouse, it removes traditional musical knowledge as a barrier: any key you press produces something that sounds and looks intentional. The constraint is built into the design. Where Spiegel used mouse position to navigate harmonic space, Brandel uses keyboard input to trigger curated sound-visual pairings — both are examples of instruments designed around *gesture* rather than notation.
